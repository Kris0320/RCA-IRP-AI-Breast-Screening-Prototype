## Key shift
The tutor's central concern is that the presentation jumps too quickly into AI and workflow analysis without first establishing the broader healthcare context. The structural problem is a narrowing-too-early sequence: the audience has not been told what the NHS is, what breast cancer screening looks like, how radiology operates, or what the patient journey is before the slides start comparing AI deployment models. All the existing content is strong, but it will not land without that foundational framing. The fix is not about adding more content — it is about reordering and expanding the front section so context, scale, and the introduction of AI as disruption each get their own moment.

## Tutor feedback

### Structure (highest priority)
- The front section is missing the broad context layer: NHS as a public health system, breast cancer screening at scale, how radiology departments operate, and what the patient journey looks like as a woman going through a mammogram.
- The sequence should move from big picture → smaller context → research → insight, not jump straight to AI models and disagreement. The audience needs to know what standard practice looks like before you can explain why AI creates new problems.
- Add one or two slides at the front that answer: what is the NHS, what is breast cancer screening, how many women are screened per year, how many hospitals, who are the people doing this work, and what does the day-to-day look like in a radiology department.
- Before showing AI workflow comparisons, explicitly qualify where AI actually is right now: there are X pilots in the NHS, no live clinical services in the UK, some live deployments in Germany and parts of Europe. This context makes the analysis make sense.
- Build the argument as a chain: standard radiology has disagreement → AI improves detection accuracy but does not fix the disagreement problem → that is the specific gap Readyology addresses. Without each link of this chain, the solution appears disconnected from the problem.
- Do not rush to the solution. Spend proportionally more time setting up the problem context so that when Readyology appears, the audience already understands why it is needed.

### Opening story
- The radiologist character in the opening story should be more specific and concrete. Give her a name, a hospital, a daily workload. For example: "Sarah, a Breast Advanced Practitioner at [NHS Trust], reviews over 80 mammograms a day. Each is a life-critical decision. When she disagrees with the AI — there is no protocol."
- Imagery should do storytelling work throughout. Add photographs of radiology rooms, mammogram scanning equipment, clinical environments, and women in the screening experience. The visual context grounds everything that follows.

### Research evidence
- Maximise the depth and breadth of the research that has already been done. Qualify it in numbers: how many interviews, how many expert sessions, how many radiologists engaged, what types of research methods were used.
- Real expert access is hard to get and gives the project credibility. Make sure the audience knows this research is grounded in real clinical opinion, not desk research alone.
- Insights should be framed as synthesis conclusions, not observations. Lock each insight to its consequence: for example, "disagreement between AI and radiologist is unaddressed → misdiagnosed cancer risk increases → this is why we are designing for this specific moment."

### Individual slides
- Value proposition slide: currently too text-dense. Add 3–4 bullet points as a takeaway summary so the key points are scannable.
- HMW slide: theory of change is strong. The full problem statement may not be necessary — consider keeping only the HMW question and letting the theory of change carry the reasoning.
- Titles should do the storytelling work. Anyone reading the slide without you in the room should understand what it is saying. Replace descriptive labels with active narrative titles.
- Emphasise and label the things you want the audience to notice on each diagram — do not assume they will read everything. Active diagrams, not passive ones.

### Closing and next steps
- The presentation currently has no answer to "what do you want to do with this?" Add a next steps or future direction slide that says where this is going: partnership with an AI company, presentation to an NHS trust, turning it into a pilot framework, or something else.
- The stakes of the project should be made explicit early: this is about breast cancer screening, early detection, and saving lives. Say that from the beginning.

### Production priorities
- Fix structure and context first. If structure is wrong, all other feedback becomes harder to address.
- Add imagery throughout — health, radiology, clinical environments, scanning.
- Finish the prototype videos. If time is tight, a strong static slide with clear content is acceptable over an unfinished video.
- Practice delivery with the slides in final form so the verbal narrative reinforces what is shown.

## Key questions raised
- What is the NHS and how does it operate as a public healthcare system?
- What does a breast cancer screening programme look like at national scale — how many women, how many hospitals, what is the detection rate?
- What does the patient experience look like if you are a woman going through a mammogram for the first time vs. recall?
- How many AI pilots are currently active in the NHS? Which countries have operational live services?
- What are the real consequences when disagreement between AI and radiologist is not handled — what happens to the patient?
- What do you want to do with Readyology after the IRP — who would you work with, where would you pilot it, what is the realistic next step?
- Is this a framework, a protocol, a software layer, or a service design proposition — and can you say that clearly in one sentence?
