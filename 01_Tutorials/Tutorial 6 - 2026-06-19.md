## Key shift
The tutor accepted the overall structure and confirmed the content is strong, but identified a fundamental narrative problem: the presentation jumps directly into workflows and analysis without landing the three story beats that make everything else make sense. The three beats are: (1) radiology as a serious, high-stakes clinical operation; (2) AI as a current inflection point technology entering health services; and (3) the specific consequences of AI applied to radiology. Without these three beats in place, slides on AI workflow comparisons, disagreement moments, and country analysis become confusing or disconnected. The session was primarily about storytelling sequence, diagram clarity, and how to frame and sell the service proposition before showing the prototype.

## Tutor feedback

### Presentation title
- Add the word "cancer" — breast cancer screening, not just breast screening. The word signals seriousness and specificity and should appear from the very first slide.

### Narrative structure (highest priority)
- Three story beats must be landed before any workflow analysis:
  1. Radiology as a clinical operation — what it is, scale, how it works, why it matters (e.g. 27,000 screenings per year in the NHS, X deaths per year from breast cancer)
  2. AI as an inflection point — AI is entering health services right now, it is in pilot across multiple countries and health systems, it is not yet standard practice, it is disruptive and exciting but also risky
  3. The effect of AI on radiology — what changes, what it enables, what issues and complications it introduces, and why this is the moment that needs designing for
- Without these beats, slides like the country-comparison AI workflow analysis make no sense. They need to come after the broader framing, not before.
- The structure should narrow progressively: big picture context → AI in health → AI in radiology → disagreement as the critical failure point → solution

### AI workflow comparison slide
- This slide currently lacks an explicit summary insight. The takeaway must be stated clearly: across all four (or ten) models, the disagreement moment is the unresolved gap.
- Consider adding a simpler introductory slide before the comparison — one that explains what an AI workflow is, what human-in-the-loop means, and what the general system logic is — so the comparison makes sense to a non-specialist audience.

### Research evidence
- The research slide needs to be qualified with numbers: how many interviews, how many hackathons, how many expert sessions, how many types of methods. Numbers make it beyond question that this is evidence-based.
- Two dimensions to communicate: (1) breadth and quantity of methods and engagements; (2) depth and quality of domain expertise accessed. Radiologist access is hard to obtain and gives the project authority.

### Insights
- Insights must be synthesis conclusions, not observations. The format should be: this is what we understood → this is the consequence → this is why we are addressing it.
- Example framing: "In all disagreement scenarios, the failure point is unresolved. When AI and radiologist disagree and no protocol exists, the risk of a missed cancer diagnosis increases. That is what we designed for."
- Segment insights by type — e.g. system/structural, human/relational, data/feedback loop — and make each one land its consequence clearly.

### Diagrams and titles
- Titles should do the storytelling work. Anyone reading the slide without a presenter should understand the point. Replace descriptive labels with narrative titles.
- All diagrams should be active, not passive: highlight and label what the audience needs to notice. Do not assume they will read everything. Use emphasis, call-outs, legend, labels, and annotations.
- In the system map, call out: radiologist interaction with AI, radiologist interaction with the service layer, reading and outcome logic, and the breast cancer pathway in the context of specialists and experts.
- In the journey map, emphasise the disagreement moment and the intervention moment. Frame it so the pain point is immediately visible.

### Service proposition and branding
- Before showing the prototype, introduce the service as a value proposition. Give it a name and frame it as a concept: what is it, what does it do, who benefits and how, how does it integrate into the existing system.
- The four questions to answer in the solution section: Why, What, How, Who.
- After introducing the proposition, then present what was tested and what was found. The sequence should be: name → explain → test → findings → next steps.

### Prototype placement
- The prototype currently appears too late. The tutor suggested introducing the product earlier and then going into detail — raise it earlier so the audience knows what you're building, then use later slides to show the testing and detail.
- Consider restructuring: introduce the concept → show the prototype interaction → go into the design system detail → present testing and validation → next steps.

### Prototype interface
- The interaction model works. The triage overlay and RAG (Red / Amber / Green) pattern works.
- Critical: the RAG colour mapping must be explicitly clear. When the audience sees amber, amber, amber, red across the screen, they must immediately understand what status each colour maps to. Add a clear legend or key.
- The neutral blue buttons work. When a card is in an amber state, the amber should visually link to the card and to the interface state so the mapping is unambiguous.
- Be explicit about the context of each interface surface: the screening arbitration surface is an operational tool used inside the radiology department during the reading moment. The case detail surface is a different context. State this.

### General
- Balance statistics and imagery. The presentation needs more photographs — clinical environments, radiology rooms, mammogram equipment, the patient experience. Imagery helps tell the story as much as data does.
- Tutor offered to run another detailed feedback tutorial before assessment. Send materials as you go so they can review in rounds.
